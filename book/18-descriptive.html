18  Descriptive and Qualitative Exploration

18.1 Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  • Move beyond df.describe() to characterize the shape, skewness, and spread of a distribution using quantiles and the coefficient of variation.
  • Identify outliers with the IQR method and the z-score method, and decide whether each outlier reflects an error or a legitimate extreme observation.
  • Produce frequency tables with .value_counts() and cross-tabulations with pd.crosstab().
  • Build a correlation matrix with df.corr() and interpret its structure.
  • Write a short descriptive summary that documents N, shape, outliers, and missingness for any new dataset.

18.2 Why Descriptive Exploration Comes First

Before you compare groups or test hypotheses, you need to understand what your data looks like. Descriptive exploration is the process of characterizing a dataset’s central tendencies, variability, shape, and outliers — building an informed mental model of the data that guides every subsequent analytical decision. This chapter goes beyond the .describe() method you have already used and introduces systematic techniques for understanding distributions, identifying unusual observations, examining categorical patterns, and constructing frequency tables and cross-tabulations.

The philosophy here is qualitative in the broadest sense: you are looking for patterns, surprises, and anomalies rather than computing formal test statistics. The goal is to know your data well enough that nothing in the later chapters catches you off guard.

batting = pd.read_csv('Batting.csv')

# Cannabis sales (cleaned)

18.5 Beyond .describe(): Understanding Distributions

The .describe() method gives you the five-number summary (min, 25th percentile, median, 75th percentile, max) plus the mean and count. This is a useful starting point, but it hides important features of a distribution: its shape, its skewness, and whether it has multiple modes.

18.5.1 Skewness

Many real-world variables are right-skewed — a long tail extends toward high values while most observations cluster near the low end. Income, city populations, and home prices all follow this pattern. Cannabis sales across Colorado counties are a clear example:

annual_sales = (
    cannabis[cannabis['year'] == 2023]
    .groupby('county')['retail']
    .sum()
    .dropna()
)

print(annual_sales.describe())
print(f'\nSkewness: {annual_sales.skew():.2f}')
print(f'Mean:     ${annual_sales.mean():,.0f}')
print(f'Median:   ${annual_sales.median():,.0f}')

When the mean is substantially larger than the median, the distribution is right-skewed, and the mean is being pulled upward by a few very large values. In this case, the median is a better measure of the “typical” county than the mean.

18.5.2 Quantiles and percentiles

Quantiles divide your data into equal-sized groups. The .quantile() method lets you compute arbitrary percentiles:

# Decile boundaries for batting average
percentiles = qualified['BA'].quantile(np.arange(0, 1.1, 0.1))
print('Batting average deciles:')
print(percentiles.round(3))

Quantiles are useful for creating bins (as with pd.qcut() in Chapter 8), for identifying where a specific value falls in the distribution, and for detecting whether the distribution has unusual gaps or concentrations.

18.5.3 The coefficient of variation

Standard deviation tells you how spread out values are, but its meaning depends on the scale of the variable. A standard deviation of 1,000 is enormous for a variable with a mean of 500 but negligible for a variable with a mean of 10,000,000. The coefficient of variation (CV) — the ratio of the standard deviation to the mean — provides a scale-free measure of relative variability:

for col in ['medical', 'retail']:
    mean_val = cannabis[col].mean()
    std_val = cannabis[col].std()
    cv = std_val / mean_val
    print(f'{col}: mean={mean_val:,.0f}, std={std_val:,.0f}, CV={cv:.2f}')

A CV above 1.0 indicates very high variability relative to the mean — typical of skewed distributions.

18.6 Identifying Outliers

An outlier is an observation that is unusually far from the rest of the data. Outliers are not automatically errors — they may be legitimate extreme values that are analytically important. The goal is to identify them, investigate them, and make a documented decision about whether to include them in your analysis.

18.6.1 The IQR method

The interquartile range (IQR) is the distance between the 25th and 75th percentiles. The standard convention defines outliers as values below Q1 − 1.5 × IQR or above Q3 + 1.5 × IQR:

def find_outliers_iqr(series, multiplier=1.5):
    """Identify outliers using the IQR method."""
    Q1 = series.quantile(0.25)
    Q3 = series.quantile(0.75)
    IQR = Q3 - Q1
    lower = Q1 - multiplier * IQR
    upper = Q3 + multiplier * IQR

    outliers = series[(series < lower) | (series > upper)]

    print(f'Q1={Q1:.3f}, Q3={Q3:.3f}, IQR={IQR:.3f}')
    print(f'Lower fence: {lower:.3f}, Upper fence: {upper:.3f}')
    print(f'Outliers: {len(outliers)} of {len(series)} ({len(outliers)/len(series):.1%})')

    return outliers

hr_outliers = find_outliers_iqr(qualified['HR'])

18.6.2 The z-score method

A z-score measures how many standard deviations a value is from the mean. Values with |z| > 3 are conventionally considered outliers:

qualified['hr_zscore'] = (
    (qualified['HR'] - qualified['HR'].mean()) / qualified['HR'].std()
)

extreme = qualified[qualified['hr_zscore'].abs() > 3]
print(f'Observations with |z| > 3: {len(extreme)}')
print(extreme[['playerID', 'yearID', 'HR', 'hr_zscore']].sort_values('hr_zscore', ascending=False).head(10))

18.6.3 Investigating outliers

Once you identify outliers, investigate them before deciding what to do. Are they data entry errors, measurement artifacts, or genuinely extreme observations?

# Are the extreme HR seasons real or errors?
# Check: do the player names and years match known record holders?
print(extreme[['playerID', 'yearID', 'teamID', 'HR']].sort_values('HR', ascending=False).head(5))

If these are legitimate records (Barry Bonds’ 73 HR in 2001, for instance), removing them would be analytically dishonest. If they are data entry errors (a player with 999 HR), removing them is necessary. Document the decision either way.

18.7 Categorical Patterns

For non-numeric columns, the primary descriptive tools are frequency tables and cross-tabulations.

18.7.1 Frequency tables

The .value_counts() method is your workhorse for categorical exploration. Use it with normalize=True to get proportions instead of counts:

# County distribution of cannabis sales records
county_counts = cannabis['county'].value_counts()
print(f'Counties with data: {len(county_counts)}')
print(f'\nTop 10:')
print(county_counts.head(10))
print(f'\nBottom 5:')
print(county_counts.tail(5))
# Age band distribution in Colorado (2020)
bins = [0, 18, 65, 101]
labels = ['Children (0-17)', 'Working Age (18-64)', 'Seniors (65+)']
est_2020 = estimates[estimates['year'] == 2020].copy()
est_2020['age_band'] = pd.cut(est_2020['age'], bins=bins, labels=labels, right=False)

band_totals = est_2020.groupby('age_band', observed=True)['totalpopulation'].sum()
print('Statewide population by age band (2020):')
print(band_totals)
print(f'\nProportions:')
print((band_totals / band_totals.sum()).round(3))

18.7.2 Cross-tabulations

A cross-tabulation (crosstab) shows the joint distribution of two categorical variables. Use pd.crosstab() to build one:

# Decades × league for batting data
batting_with_league = batting.copy()

decade_counts = pd.crosstab(
    qualified['decade'],
    qualified['teamID'].str[:2],  # rough proxy
    margins=True
)
print('Player-seasons by decade (first 5 decades):')
print(decade_counts.head())

Cross-tabulations become more informative with normalize to show proportions and with margins=True to include row and column totals.

18.8 Summarizing Multiple Variables Together

When you have several numeric columns, examining them individually is tedious. A correlation matrix provides a compact summary of pairwise relationships:

batting_metrics = qualified[['AB', 'H', 'HR', 'BB', 'BA', 'SLG', 'OBP', 'OPS']].copy()

corr_matrix = batting_metrics.corr().round(2)
print(corr_matrix)

We will visualize this matrix as a heatmap in Chapter 19 and test individual correlations formally in Chapter 20.

18.9 The Descriptive Summary Template

For any new dataset or analytical question, produce a descriptive summary that covers these elements. You can structure this as a Markdown cell in your notebook:

## Descriptive Summary: [Dataset / Variable]

**Unit of analysis:** [what each row represents]
**N:** [number of observations after filtering]
**Key statistics:** mean, median, std, min, max
**Shape:** [symmetric, right-skewed, bimodal, etc.]
**Outliers:** [count, method used, decision made]
**Missing data:** [count, pattern, impact on analysis]
**Notable patterns:** [any surprises, clusters, or anomalies]

18.10 Common Issues in This Chapter

WarningThe mean looks “off” but the data is fine

What it means: The distribution is heavily skewed. A few extreme values pull the mean in one direction, even though the rest of the data sits in a narrow range.

How to fix it: Report the median alongside the mean, and name the shape explicitly (“right-skewed”). The median is the better “typical” measure for skewed variables like income or sales.

WarningYour outlier detector flagged half the data

What it means: The 1.5 × IQR or |z| > 3 rule assumes a roughly symmetric distribution. On highly skewed data, both methods produce far too many “outliers.”

How to fix it: Log-transform the variable (np.log1p) before applying outlier rules, or use a more robust method like median absolute deviation (MAD). Investigate what “unusual” means in the domain before applying a mechanical rule.

Warningcrosstab margins look wrong

What it means: Missing values are excluded from crosstab by default, so the marginal totals may not match len(df).

How to fix it: Pass dropna=False to include NaN as its own category, or pre-fill missing values with a sentinel like 'Unknown' before crosstabbing. Document the choice.

WarningA correlation is high but the relationship is noise

What it means: Correlation summarizes a monotonic trend. A strong correlation can hide outliers, nonlinearity, or a relationship driven by a handful of points.

How to fix it: Always visualize before concluding. A scatter plot or pairplot will show what the correlation coefficient cannot. See Chapter 19.

18.11 Key Takeaways

  • Descriptive exploration is about building intuition — shape, skew, outliers, missingness — before any formal comparison.
  • The mean lies when the distribution is skewed. Always check the median, and name the shape out loud.
  • Outlier detection is a starting point, not an answer. Every outlier deserves an investigation and a documented decision.
  • .value_counts(), pd.crosstab(), and .corr() are three tools that cover most categorical-and-multivariate exploration.
  • Summarize every new dataset with the descriptive summary template before moving on. It forces you to answer the “what is this data?” question explicitly.


```{python}
# Age band distribution in Colorado (2020)
bins = [0, 18, 65, 101]
labels = ['Children (0-17)', 'Working Age (18-64)', 'Seniors (65+)']
est_2020 = estimates[estimates['year'] == 2020].copy()
est_2020['age_band'] = pd.cut(est_2020['age'], bins=bins, labels=labels, right=False)

band_totals = est_2020.groupby('age_band', observed=True)['totalpopulation'].sum()
print('Statewide population by age band (2020):')
print(band_totals)
print(f'\nProportions:')
print((band_totals / band_totals.sum()).round(3))
```

### Cross-tabulations

A cross-tabulation (crosstab) shows the joint distribution of two categorical variables. Use `pd.crosstab()` to build one:

```{python}
# Decades × league for batting data
batting_with_league = batting.copy()

decade_counts = pd.crosstab(
    qualified['decade'],
    qualified['teamID'].str[:2],  # rough proxy
    margins=True
)
print('Player-seasons by decade (first 5 decades):')
print(decade_counts.head())
```

Cross-tabulations become more informative with `normalize` to show proportions and with `margins=True` to include row and column totals.

When you have several numeric columns, examining them individually is tedious. A **correlation matrix** provides a compact summary of pairwise relationships:

```{python}
batting_metrics = qualified[['AB', 'H', 'HR', 'BB', 'BA', 'SLG', 'OBP', 'OPS']].copy()

```

## The Descriptive Summary Template

For any new dataset or analytical question, produce a descriptive summary that covers these elements. You can structure this as a Markdown cell in your notebook:

```markdown
## Descriptive Summary: [Dataset / Variable]

**Unit of analysis:** [what each row represents]
**N:** [number of observations after filtering]
**Key statistics:** mean, median, std, min, max
**Shape:** [symmetric, right-skewed, bimodal, etc.]

```

## Common Issues in This Chapter

::: {.callout-warning}
## The mean looks "off" but the data is fine
**What it means:** The distribution is heavily skewed. A few extreme values pull the mean in one direction, even though the rest of the data sits in a narrow range.

**How to fix it:** Report the median alongside the mean, and name the shape explicitly ("right-skewed"). The median is the better "typical" measure for skewed variables like income or sales.
:::

::: {.callout-warning}
## Your outlier detector flagged half the data
**What it means:** The 1.5 × IQR or |z| > 3 rule assumes a roughly symmetric distribution. On highly skewed data, both methods produce far too many "outliers."

**How to fix it:** Log-transform the variable (`np.log1p`) before applying outlier rules, or use a more robust method like median absolute deviation (MAD). Investigate what "unusual" means in the domain before applying a mechanical rule.
:::

::: {.callout-warning}
## `crosstab` margins look wrong
**What it means:** Missing values are excluded from `crosstab` by default, so the marginal totals may not match `len(df)`.

**How to fix it:** Pass `dropna=False` to include `NaN` as its own category, or pre-fill missing values with a sentinel like `'Unknown'` before crosstabbing. Document the choice.
:::

::: {.callout-warning}
## A correlation is high but the relationship is noise
**What it means:** Correlation summarizes a monotonic trend. A strong correlation can hide outliers, nonlinearity, or a relationship driven by a handful of points.

**How to fix it:** Always visualize before concluding. A scatter plot or pairplot will show what the correlation coefficient cannot. See @sec-visualization.
:::

## Key Takeaways

- Descriptive exploration is about building intuition — shape, skew, outliers, missingness — before any formal comparison.
- The mean lies when the distribution is skewed. Always check the median, and name the shape out loud.
- Outlier detection is a starting point, not an answer. Every outlier deserves an investigation and a documented decision.
- `.value_counts()`, `pd.crosstab()`, and `.corr()` are three tools that cover most categorical-and-multivariate exploration.
- Summarize every new dataset with the **descriptive summary template** before moving on. It forces you to answer the "what is this data?" question explicitly.
