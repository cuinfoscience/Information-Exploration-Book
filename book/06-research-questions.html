6  Research Questions and Study Designs

6.1 Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  • Translate a vague curiosity into a specific, measurable research question using the “outcome across groups / time / conditions” template.
  • Identify the unit of analysis for a dataset and recognize common mistakes that flow from misunderstanding it.
  • Distinguish between-subjects from within-subjects comparisons and cross-sectional from longitudinal analyses.
  • Explain why observational data supports associations but not causal claims, and name common confounds in public datasets.

6.2 Why This Chapter Comes Before the Code

The technical chapters ahead teach you to load, clean, extend, and reshape data. Those are essential mechanical skills. But mechanics alone do not produce insight — you also need to ask the right questions and understand what your data can and cannot tell you. This chapter introduces the conceptual vocabulary for formulating research questions with tabular data, recognizing the structure of the comparisons your data supports, and identifying the limitations that prevent you from drawing conclusions the data does not warrant.

Reading this chapter first — before writing a line of pandas or SQL — gives you a frame for the technical work ahead. The datasets you will use, and the analyses you will eventually run, are shaped by the questions you are trying to answer.

None of this requires new code. It requires new thinking.

6.3 What Makes a Good Research Question?

A good research question is specific enough to be answered with data and interesting enough to be worth answering. Compare these two questions:

  • “Is baseball different now?” — too vague to answer. Different how? Compared to when?
  • “Has the league-wide home run rate per at-bat changed between the 1990s and the 2010s?” — specific, measurable, and answerable with the Lahman batting data.

A useful template is: “Is there a difference in [outcome] between [groups] / over [time] / across [conditions]?” The components of this template — outcome, groups, time, conditions — map directly to columns in your data. If you cannot point to specific columns that represent each component, the question is not yet answerable with the data you have.

6.4 The Unit of Analysis

Before you can answer any question, you need to know what a single row in your dataset represents. This is the unit of analysis, and getting it wrong is one of the most common sources of error in data work.

In the Lahman batting data, each row is a player-season-stint: one player’s statistics for one team in one season. If you compute the average batting average across all rows, you are computing the average across player-seasons — which weights a player with 10 at-bats the same as a player with 600. That is almost certainly not what you intend.

In the Colorado population data, each row is a county-year-age: one county’s population for one single year of age in one year. If you sum totalpopulation without grouping by county and year first, you will add up every age group and get a number far larger than any county’s actual population.

The discipline from Parts II and III — always checking .shape, always understanding what a row represents — is more than a cleaning habit. It is the foundation of valid analysis.

Dataset Unit of analysis Common mistake
Lahman Batting Player-team-season Averaging rates without weighting by at-bats
DOLA Population County-year-age Summing population without grouping by county-year
Cannabis Sales County-month-year Treating monthly data as annual without aggregation
CDC Deaths County-year-age group-cause Double-counting deaths across cause categories

6.5 Between-Subjects vs. Within-Subjects Comparisons

Most analytical questions involve comparisons, and the structure of the comparison determines which methods are appropriate.

Between-subjects (or between-groups) comparisons look at different entities measured under different conditions. “Do urban counties have higher crime rates than rural counties?” compares different counties — each county is measured once, and the comparison is across groups. In tabular data, this means filtering to different subsets of rows and comparing an outcome column.

Within-subjects (or repeated-measures) comparisons look at the same entity measured at multiple time points or under multiple conditions. “Has Boulder County’s dependency ratio changed from 2000 to 2020?” tracks a single county across time. In tabular data, this means filtering to one entity and examining how an outcome column changes across another column (usually time).

The distinction matters because between-subjects comparisons are confounded by all the ways the groups differ beyond the variable of interest. Urban and rural counties differ in population density, but they also differ in demographics, economic base, healthcare access, and dozens of other factors. A within-subjects comparison controls for many of these differences (Boulder County’s geography does not change between 2000 and 2020), but introduces the possibility that other things changed simultaneously (state policy, national trends).

6.5.1 Recognizing the structure in your data

Ask yourself: “Am I comparing different entities, or the same entity at different times?”

import pandas as pd

6.6 Cross-Sectional vs. Longitudinal Analysis

A closely related distinction is whether your analysis examines one point in time or multiple points.

Cross-sectional analysis looks at a snapshot: all counties in 2020, all players in the 2022 season. It is good for describing the current state of things and identifying patterns across entities, but it cannot tell you about change over time.

Longitudinal analysis tracks the same entities across time: Boulder County from 1990 to 2024, league-wide batting averages from 1900 to 2022. It can reveal trends, turning points, and the effects of interventions (a new policy, a rule change), but it requires careful handling of time-related confounds.

Most of the datasets in this book support both types of analysis. The cannabis sales data can be analyzed cross-sectionally (which counties had the highest sales in 2023?) or longitudinally (how have Denver’s sales changed since legalization?). The choice depends on the question, not the data.

6.7 Observational Data and the Limits of Causation

Every dataset in this book is observational — the data was collected by recording what happened, not by experimentally manipulating conditions and measuring the effect. This is the norm in data analysis outside of controlled experiments, and it imposes a fundamental limitation: observational data can show associations, but it cannot prove causation.

Consider the merged Lahman dataset from Chapter 11, where you found that higher-paid players tend to have better batting statistics. Can you conclude that paying players more causes them to hit better? No. Teams pay more for players who have already demonstrated high performance. The causal arrow likely runs from performance to salary, not the other way around. And both might be influenced by a third factor — age, position, market size — that you have not accounted for.

This does not mean observational analysis is useless. It means you must be disciplined about the language you use. Say “counties with higher population density tend to have higher crime rates” rather than “high population density causes higher crime rates.” The first is a description of a pattern in the data. The second is a causal claim that the data cannot support.

6.7.1 Common confounds in the course datasets

Apparent relationship Possible confound
Higher salary → better batting stats Teams pay for past performance; age and experience drive both
More cannabis sales → more crime Both correlate with county population; urban counties have more of both
Higher dependency ratio → lower income Rural counties have both older populations and less economic diversity
More deaths → larger county Death counts scale with population; rates are the meaningful comparison

The last example deserves emphasis. Comparing raw counts across groups of different sizes is one of the most common analytical mistakes. Denver will always have more deaths, more crimes, and more cannabis sales than San Juan County — because Denver has 700,000 people and San Juan has 700. The meaningful comparison is the rate: deaths per 100,000 population, crimes per capita, sales per resident. Computing rates requires the population data, which is why the multi-table join in Chapter 15 is more than a technical exercise — it is an analytical necessity.

6.8 Formulating Your Question

Before writing any analytical code, answer these four questions in a Markdown cell:

  1. What is the outcome I am measuring? (Which column? What does a higher or lower value mean?)
  2. What am I comparing? (Groups? Time periods? Conditions?)
  3. What is the unit of analysis? (What does each row represent? Do I need to aggregate first?)
  4. What can I conclude vs. what can I only speculate about? (Association or causation? What confounds exist?)

This takes two minutes and prevents hours of misguided analysis. The remaining chapters in Part IV assume you have done this work before running any code.

6.9 Common Issues in This Chapter

WarningYour research question is too broad to answer

What it means: You wrote something like “Is housing broken?” — there is no specific column you can point to as the outcome, and no clear group you are comparing.

How to fix it: Rewrite using the “outcome across groups / time / conditions” template. Force yourself to name the specific column that represents the outcome and the specific filter that defines the groups. If you cannot, the question is not yet answerable with the data you have.

WarningYour summary statistic does not mean what you think it means

What it means: You computed a mean, median, or sum without accounting for the unit of analysis. Averaging batting averages across player-seasons, or summing population across age groups, gives a number that sounds meaningful but is not.

How to fix it: Before any aggregation, write down what a single row represents. Decide whether you need to aggregate to a different unit first. If you are averaging rates, decide whether to weight by the underlying sample size.

WarningYou compared raw counts across unequal populations

What it means: Denver has more of everything than San Juan County. Conclusions based on raw counts are really conclusions about population size.

How to fix it: Convert to per-capita rates before comparing. “Deaths per 100,000 population” is almost always the right denominator for cross-county comparisons.

WarningYou slipped into causal language

What it means: Your write-up says “X causes Y” when the data only shows that X and Y are correlated.

How to fix it: Reread your conclusions and replace causal verbs (causes, leads to, drives) with correlational language (is associated with, tends to co-occur with). Name at least one plausible confound explicitly so the reader knows you considered it.

6.10 Key Takeaways

  • A good research question names a specific outcome, a specific comparison, and a specific unit of analysis.
  • The unit of analysis — what a single row represents — determines which aggregations are meaningful. Checking .shape is more than hygiene; it is analytical discipline.
  • Between-subjects vs. within-subjects and cross-sectional vs. longitudinal are two independent distinctions. Know which you are doing before you start.
  • Observational data supports associations, not causal claims. Use language that matches what your data can actually show.
  • Per-capita rates, not raw counts, make cross-group comparisons meaningful.
